The I&T Plan and
Program Architecture

How to structure a spacecraft integration and test campaign — from first hardware to launch readiness, and from piece parts to observatory.

Every spacecraft I&T campaign begins the same way: with a blank sheet of paper and a question. Given what this spacecraft has to do, given the environment it has to survive, given the schedule and budget available — what exactly do we need to build, integrate, and test, in what order, and how do we know when we're done?

The document that answers that question is the I&T Plan. It is, in a very real sense, the constitution of the entire test program. It establishes the philosophy, defines the scope, describes the approach, and allocates responsibility. A good I&T Plan anticipates the hard decisions before they become emergencies. A poor one — or the absence of one — means that every hard decision gets made under schedule pressure, without the benefit of prior deliberation.

This article explains how I&T campaigns are structured: the phases of the program, the levels of integration from the smallest piece part to a complete observatory, the types of tests performed at each level, and the governing documents and reviews that hold the whole architecture together. Understanding this structure is a prerequisite for everything that follows in this series.

"The I&T Plan is not a schedule. It is not a procedure. It is the engineering argument for why your test program is sufficient to justify flight. It should be written — and challenged — with that standard in mind."

1. What the I&T Plan Must Accomplish

Before examining what goes into an I&T Plan, it is worth being precise about what the document must accomplish. An I&T Plan is not a schedule, though it informs one. It is not a test procedure, though it defines the framework from which procedures are derived. It is an engineering argument — a structured case that the test program, taken as a whole, is sufficient to verify that the spacecraft meets its requirements and is ready to fly.

That argument must address four fundamental questions:

  • What are we trying to prove? — The test program must be traceable to mission and system requirements. Every test must exist because something needs to be verified. Tests performed "because we always do this" without a clear requirement link are not necessarily wrong, but they cannot be justified when schedule pressure comes — and schedule pressure always comes.
  • How will we prove it? — This covers the approach: which tests are performed at which integration levels, which test environments are used, what instrumentation is required, what constitutes a pass/fail criterion.
  • What flight configuration will be tested? — The principle of test-as-you-fly demands that the configuration under test be as close as possible to the flight configuration. The I&T Plan must identify and justify any deviations.
  • How will we know we are done? — The Plan defines the completion criteria: what data must be collected, what anomalies must be resolved, what reviews must be held, before the spacecraft can be declared ready for launch.

2. The Levels of Integration — From Parts to Observatory

The most important structural concept in any I&T program is the integration level hierarchy. Spacecraft hardware is assembled in stages, and each stage has its own integration and test requirements. Understanding this hierarchy is not merely academic — it determines the sequence of the entire program, the allocation of test resources, and the strategy for finding problems early, when they are cheapest and easiest to fix.

The hierarchy described below reflects the full range of complexity, from a simple single-instrument spacecraft to a large multi-instrument observatory. Not every program has every level — a small CubeSat mission might compress several levels together. A flagship-class observatory like the James Webb Space Telescope has every level and more. The principle is the same regardless of scale: build upward from the simplest elements, verify at each level before proceeding to the next, and never assume that passing tests at a lower level guarantees performance at a higher one.

⚙ Why Levels Matter

Integration creates emergent behaviors. Two circuit boards that each pass their individual acceptance tests can still fail when connected, due to impedance mismatches, ground loops, or software timing issues that only appear in the combined system. Two subsystems that each pass thermal-vacuum testing in isolation can interact thermally in unexpected ways when mounted together on the spacecraft bus.

This is not a theoretical concern — it is the lived experience of every engineer who has worked a spacecraft I&T program. Testing at each level is the only way to find these interactions before they become on-orbit anomalies.

L0 ⚛️
Piece Parts & Materials
Foundation Parts Engineering
At the base of the hierarchy are individual materials and piece parts: electronic components (resistors, capacitors, integrated circuits), fasteners, adhesives, coatings, lubricants, and raw structural materials. For flight programs, piece parts are typically subject to parts screening — a program of testing and inspection designed to identify early failures ("infant mortality") before parts are incorporated into assemblies.
Electronic parts used on spacecraft are typically procured to controlled specifications (such as the MIL-PRF series) and may be subject to destructive physical analysis (DPA) on lot samples to verify internal construction. Problems found here — a bad lot of capacitors, a counterfeit material, a lubricant incompatible with the thermal environment — are infinitely cheaper to address at this level than after they have been incorporated into a flight assembly.
Typical I&T Activities Incoming inspection · Lot acceptance testing · Radiation testing · Destructive physical analysis (DPA) · Material certifications
L1 🔧
Assemblies & Circuit Card Assemblies (CCAs)
Manufacturing Workmanship
At Level 1, piece parts are assembled into functional units: populated circuit card assemblies (CCAs), bonded structural panels, small mechanisms, and harness segments. I&T activities here focus on workmanship verification and basic functionality. Circuit cards are subjected to optical and often X-ray inspection to verify solder joint quality and component placement.
Spacecraft electronics are typically required to meet NASA, Military or International standards such as IPC-A-610 Class 3 workmanship standards, intended for high-reliability electronics where continued performance is critical. For circuit cards destined for optical instruments or sensitive detectors, contamination control begins here.
Typical I&T Activities X-ray inspection · Electrical continuity · Dimensional inspection · Workmanship audit
L2 📦
Components & Electronics Units
Component I&T Environmental Test Qual vs. Acceptance
At Level 2, assemblies are integrated into complete electronic units, mechanisms, and structural assemblies: a flight computer, a power conditioning and distribution unit (PCDU), a reaction wheel assembly, a star tracker, a propulsion system component. This is the first level at which comprehensive environmental testing is typically performed.
Component-level testing serves two purposes: it screens out latent workmanship defects (the burn-in function), and it verifies the unit can survive launch and the space environment (the qualification function). This is where the critical distinction between qualification testing (on a qual unit, at elevated levels) and acceptance testing (on every flight unit, at flight levels) is most sharply drawn — a distinction with profound implications for program cost and schedule.
Typical I&T Activities Comprehensive functional testing · Vibration testing · Thermal cycling & thermal-vacuum · EMC testing · Mass properties verification
L3 🔌
Subsystems
Interface Verification Functional
At Level 3, components are integrated into functional subsystems: the Electrical Power Subsystem (EPS), the Attitude Determination and Control Subsystem (ADCS), the Command and Data Handling subsystem (C&DH), the Telecommunications subsystem, the Propulsion subsystem, and the Thermal Control subsystem.
Subsystem-level I&T is where interface verification between components becomes the primary focus. Individual components may have each passed their acceptance tests, but when connected — electrically, mechanically, and thermally — new behaviors emerge. The power subsystem must deliver clean, regulated power to all loads under all load conditions. The ADCS must close its control loops with the flight software. Programs that skip formal subsystem testing find these problems at the spacecraft level, under more pressure, in a more complex configuration.
Typical I&T Activities Interface verification · Closed-loop functional testing · Subsystem thermal-vacuum · Software integration testing
L4 🔭
Instruments & Payload
Science Payload Calibration Compatibility
Science instruments, hosted payloads, and other mission-specific hardware occupy a parallel track in the integration hierarchy. An instrument or payload is typically developed by a separate team — often at a different institution — and delivered to the spacecraft integrator as a completed, tested unit with its own I&T heritage.
The interface between instrument and spacecraft bus is one of the highest-risk interfaces in the entire program. Mechanical, electrical, thermal, and data interfaces must all be verified — ideally using a fit check (mechanical interface verification with a mass model) and a compatibility test (electrical interface verification) well before the instrument is delivered for spacecraft integration. Instrument/payload calibration — the process by which required performance is characterized — is intimately linked to I&T and must be planned as part of the overall program architecture.
Typical I&T Activities Instrument/Payload-level functional testing · Calibration campaigns · Fit checks · Electrical compatibility testing · Contamination monitoring
⚠ The Instrument/Payload Delivery Problem

Late instrument delivery is one of the most common causes of spacecraft I&T schedule compression. The pattern is familiar: the instrument slips, the spacecraft integration schedule is held, and then both are compressed together to hit the launch date — placing the program under maximum pressure at exactly the moment requiring maximum care.

The correct response is to identify early which I&T activities are schedule-critical, which have float, and which can be deferred to the launch site. This planning must be done before the instrument slips — not after.

L5 🛸
Spacecraft
System-Level I&T Full Environmental Suite
At Level 5, subsystems, instruments, and payload elements are integrated into the complete spacecraft — the vehicle on the test floor, surrounded by ground support equipment, with technicians in cleanroom garments at every access panel. Multiple subsystems must be assembled in a carefully controlled sequence determined by access requirements, contamination sensitivity, and interface dependencies.
Spacecraft-level testing is where the payoff of the entire lower-level test program is realized — or where its gaps become apparent. Problems that escaped detection at component and subsystem level are found here, in the most time-consuming test environment of the campaign. Every problem found at the subsystem level is one that does not have to be found on the spacecraft.
Typical I&T Activities System functional testing · Mass properties measurement · System-level EMC · Vibration & acoustic testing · Thermal-vacuum · End-to-end RF communications · Propulsion leak testing
L6 🌌
Observatory
Flagship Class Deployables Cryo-Optical
For the most complex space science missions — large observatories, flagship planetary spacecraft, and multi-element systems — there is a level above the spacecraft. An observatory typically consists of a spacecraft bus, multiple science instruments/payloads, perhaps a sunshield or mission-enabling structure, and deployable elements that cannot be fully tested in their flight configuration on the ground.
The James Webb Space Telescope is the canonical example. JWST comprised an optical telescope assembly (OTA), an integrated science instrument module (ISIM) containing four science instruments, a spacecraft bus, and a sunshield roughly the size of a tennis court — all folded for launch and deployed in sequence at L2. No facility on Earth was large enough to test the fully deployed observatory in a flight-like thermal-vacuum environment. The I&T program had to be designed around this fundamental constraint, using a combination of analysis, subsystem testing, and limited system-level testing to build confidence in flight performance. JWST launched in December 2021 and has exceeded its science performance requirements on orbit.
Typical I&T Activities Deployment testing with gravity offload · Cryo-optical testing · Observatory-level thermal-vacuum · Multi-element interface verification · Configuration management at maximum complexity
✦ Observatory-Level Challenges — The JWST Example

Deployable structures: The sunshield and mirror deployment sequences could be tested on the ground, but only with gravity-offload systems that introduce their own uncertainties. The test program had to characterize and account for those uncertainties explicitly.

Cryogenic testing: JWST's instruments operate at temperatures as low as 7 Kelvin. The only facility large enough to thermal-vacuum test the telescope and instruments in this configuration was Chamber A at NASA Johnson Space Center — requiring significant modification from its Apollo-era configuration.

Configuration control: With a program spanning more than two decades of development, maintaining precise knowledge of the as-tested vs. as-flown configuration across thousands of components required extraordinary configuration management discipline — a lesson applicable to any long-duration program.

3. The Phases of an I&T Campaign

An I&T campaign does not begin when the first hardware arrives on the test floor. It begins during the design phase of the program, when the I&T approach is first defined, and it ends — formally — at the launch readiness review that authorizes the spacecraft to fly. The campaign progresses through a sequence of phases corresponding to the NASA mission lifecycle; programs under other agencies follow essentially the same logical progression.

A common and costly misreading of the phase structure is to treat I&T as a Phase D activity — to staff the function late, to defer the planning, and to assume that experienced test personnel can be recruited and made effective on short notice once hardware begins to arrive. Forty years of experience says otherwise. The decisions that determine the quality of a Phase D campaign are made in Phases A, B, and C: the test philosophy, the EGSE architecture, the verification approach, the facility selections, the staffing of a team with genuine I&T expertise.

In my experience, when that expertise is absent in the early phases, one of two things happens. Decisions get made anyway — by subsystem and component engineers who are excellent at what they do but whose perspective is necessarily bounded by their piece of the system, not by the integrated whole. Or decisions don't get made at all, because no one in the room has the cross-program visibility to recognize that a decision is required. Both failure modes are invisible at the time and expensive to discover on the test floor.

A program that defers I&T investment does not save time or money — it borrows against Phase D at a very high rate of interest. By the time hardware arrives on the test floor, it is too late to correct the consequences of decisions that should have been made at the design table.

Phase Name Key I&T Activities Gate / Exit Criteria If Deferred to Phase D
Phase A Concept Study I&T concept defined; test philosophy established; top-level requirements derived from mission requirements; preliminary assessment of test facility needs Preliminary I&T Plan approved; philosophy reviewed at SRR/SDR Test philosophy undefined; requirements written without regard to how they will be verified; unverifiable requirements discovered too late to change
Phase B Preliminary Design I&T Plan baselined; integration levels and test flow defined; EGSE concept developed; test facility selection initiated; long-lead test equipment identified I&T Plan baselined at PDR; EGSE procurement authorized EGSE not ready when hardware arrives; test facility contracts placed too late; long-lead items unavailable; integration sequence not defined until hardware is already on the floor
Phase C Final Design Detailed test procedures developed; EGSE fabricated and tested; test facility contracts awarded; component and subsystem test campaigns begin Test procedures reviewed at CDR; EGSE qualification complete Test procedures written under schedule pressure with no margin for review; designs frozen with untestable features; subsystem problems not found until system-level integration
Phase D Integration & Test All integration activities; component, subsystem, and system-level environmental testing; anomaly resolution; launch campaign; launch site I&T System-level test complete; all anomalies dispositioned; LRR passed The bill comes due. Every deferred decision, every absent expert, every untested interface becomes an anomaly, a schedule slip, or a launch risk — at the worst possible time and the highest possible cost
Phase E Operations Launch and early operations (LEOP); initial on-orbit checkout; instrument commissioning; performance verification against pre-launch baseline Mission operations readiness review; science commissioning complete On-orbit anomalies with no ground test baseline to compare against; commissioning surprises that thorough I&T would have found; compressed margin for recovery

Phase D is the heart of the I&T campaign — the period when all the planning done in Phases A through C is executed. But the quality of Phase D is almost entirely determined by the quality of the planning that preceded it. Programs that arrive at Phase D with a well-developed I&T Plan, mature EGSE, and thoroughly tested lower-level hardware move through system-level integration and test with manageable disruption. Programs that arrive with deferred problems and immature test infrastructure discover, painfully, that the test floor is a very expensive place to resolve issues that should have been addressed earlier.

4. The Governing Documents

A spacecraft I&T campaign generates and consumes a substantial body of documentation. New engineers sometimes experience this as an overwhelming bureaucratic burden. With experience, it becomes clear that the documentation architecture is the engineering memory of the program — every test performed, every anomaly found, every configuration change made, captured in a way that supports traceability, retrievability, and institutional memory.

DocumentPurpose
I&T Plan Top-level document defining the I&T philosophy, scope, approach, and organization. The "constitution" of the test program.
Test Requirements Document (TRD) Traces each test requirement to its source mission or system requirement. Provides the formal basis for the test program.
Interface Control Document (ICD) Defines the mechanical, electrical, thermal, and data interfaces between subsystems and between the spacecraft and ground systems.
Test Procedures Step-by-step instructions for performing each test. Must be reviewed and approved before execution; cannot be deviated from without a formal change process.
Test Reports Formal record of each test campaign: objectives, configuration, results, anomalies, and disposition. The permanent record that a test occurred and what it showed.
Nonconformance Reports (NCRs) Formal documentation of any hardware or software condition that does not meet its requirements. Every NCR must be formally closed before flight.
Discrepancy Reports (DRs) Documentation of anomalies observed during test operations — unexpected behaviors, out-of-limit readings, test failures. The formal problem-tracking system.
Configuration Status Accounting (CSA) The formal record of the as-built and as-tested configuration of every flight hardware item. Enables precise comparison of the test configuration to the flight configuration.
Launch Readiness Matrix (LRM) The pre-launch checklist: every required test, every open NCR/DR, every open action item — tracked to closure as a condition of launch authorization.

"An anomaly that is not written down does not exist in the program record. An anomaly that is written down but not formally closed is an open risk at launch. The documentation discipline of a test program is a direct reflection of its engineering culture."

5. The Technical Reviews

At each major transition in the I&T campaign, a formal technical review is conducted. These reviews are not rubber stamps — they are structured examinations of readiness to proceed, conducted by a board that includes personnel independent of the performing team. The reviews serve two functions: they force the program to demonstrate readiness against defined criteria, and they provide an external check on the program's self-assessment.

  • System Requirements Review (SRR) — Confirms mission requirements are understood and the top-level I&T approach is consistent with them. Held in Phase A.
  • Preliminary Design Review (PDR) — Reviews the preliminary I&T Plan and the test requirements baseline. Authorizes development of detailed test procedures and EGSE. Held in Phase B.
  • Critical Design Review (CDR) — Reviews the detailed I&T Plan, test procedures, and EGSE design. Authorizes fabrication of flight hardware and procurement of test facilities. Held in Phase C.
  • Test Readiness Review (TRR) — Conducted before each major test campaign to confirm the test configuration is correct, procedures are approved, the facility is certified, and the team is prepared. A TRR is a launch condition — you cannot start a major test without one.
  • Pre-Ship Review (PSR) — Conducted before the spacecraft is shipped to the launch site. Confirms all home-facility I&T activities are complete, all anomalies are dispositioned, and the spacecraft is in its approved flight/shipping configuration.
  • Launch Readiness Review (LRR) — The final gate before launch. Reviews the complete I&T campaign, confirms all open items are closed or dispositioned, and formally authorizes the launch. Covered in depth in Article 16.
💡 On the Value of Independent Reviews

Every program believes it knows its own hardware better than any outside reviewer. This is correct, and it misses the point. The value of an independent review board is not that reviewers know the hardware better than the team. It is that they have not been living with the program long enough to have normalized its anomalies, rationalized its risks, or stopped questioning its assumptions.

Fresh eyes catch things that familiarity obscures. The programs that treat their review boards as adversaries to be managed are the ones that most need the reviews to work.

6. Ground Support Equipment — The Invisible Infrastructure

No discussion of I&T program architecture is complete without addressing Ground Support Equipment (GSE). GSE is the infrastructure — mechanical, electrical, and software — that makes spacecraft integration and test possible. It includes fixtures that support the spacecraft during handling and test, electrical systems that simulate the space environment, and software systems that command the spacecraft, acquire its telemetry, and evaluate its performance during test.

GSE can be divided into four main categories:

  • Mechanical GSE (MGSE) — Lifting fixtures, transportation containers, handling dollies, test stands, alignment fixtures, and specialized tooling required to assemble and disassemble flight hardware. MGSE must be designed to the same standard of care as flight hardware — a dropped spacecraft is not recoverable.
  • Electrical GSE (EGSE) — The electrical systems that power, command, and monitor the spacecraft during ground testing. EGSE includes spacecraft simulators, power supplies, command and telemetry processors, and the software that runs them. EGSE is the "ground version" of the spacecraft's operational environment — it must simulate the spacecraft's interfaces with fidelity sufficient to make test results meaningful.
  • Software GSE (SWGSE) — The software systems used to control test campaigns, process telemetry, evaluate performance against requirements, and manage test data. Modern SWGSE platforms provide automated test execution, real-time limit checking, and data archiving.
  • Special Purpose GSE — Depending on the mission, additional specialized equipment may be needed. For example, simulators designed to replicate signal inputs (X-rays for a detector, targets for a weapon, grappling fixtures for a robot, star field simulators for attitude control sensors)

EGSE deserves particular emphasis. The fidelity of EGSE directly determines the fidelity of the test program. An EGSE that does not accurately simulate/stimulate the spacecraft's flight interfaces will produce test results that do not accurately predict flight performance. This is not a subtle point — it has been the root cause of major on-orbit anomalies on multiple programs. The design, development, and verification of EGSE must be treated as an engineering program in its own right, with the same rigor applied to it as to the flight hardware itself.

EGSE development must begin early — in Phase B — because EGSE must be ready before the first component-level tests begin. Programs that defer EGSE development discover that they are trying to test flight hardware with immature test equipment, which is exactly backwards from any rational risk posture.

7. Building the I&T Plan: Practical Guidance

For an engineer writing or contributing to an I&T Plan for the first time, the following guidance reflects lessons learned across multiple programs and multiple decades.

Start With Requirements, Not Test Ideas

The most common error in I&T planning is starting with a list of tests and then trying to find requirements that justify them, rather than starting with requirements and deriving the tests that verify them. Requirements-driven test planning produces a test program with clear justification for every element, unambiguous pass/fail criteria, and complete supporting evidence and data. Test-idea-driven planning produces a test program that is hard to defend when schedule pressure demands cuts.

Plan for Anomalies

Every I&T campaign will have anomalies. The question is not whether anomalies will occur, but whether the program has the process infrastructure to find them, document them, investigate them, and close them in a controlled way. The I&T Plan must describe the anomaly management process: what constitutes an anomaly, how anomalies are classified, what investigation process applies to each class, and what authority is required to close each class of anomaly. Programs that do not plan for anomaly management find themselves improvising the process in the middle of a test campaign, under schedule pressure.

Identify the Schedule-Critical Path

The I&T schedule has a critical path — a sequence of activities that determines the earliest possible completion date. The I&T Plan must identify this path, because it is the path that must be protected. Every resource allocation decision, every risk management decision, every anomaly disposition decision will be made in the context of its impact on the critical path. If you do not know what the critical path is, you cannot manage to it.

Configuration Control Is Not Optional

The I&T Plan must define how the as-built and as-tested configuration of the spacecraft will be controlled and documented. Configuration control is not a quality assurance function imposed on the engineering team — it is an engineering function performed by the engineering team. The ability to answer the question "what exactly are we testing right now?" is essential to anomaly investigation, to launch readiness assessment, and to on-orbit anomaly support. Programs that treat configuration control as a paperwork burden pay for that attitude when they need to answer that question under pressure.

⚡ A Word on Schedule Pressure

Every I&T program experiences schedule pressure. It is not an aberration — it is the normal condition of spacecraft development. The question is not how to avoid it but how to respond to it without compromising flight safety.

The I&T Plan should be written with this in mind: every element should be defensible under pressure. When a program manager asks "can we skip this test?", the answer should not be "the plan says we have to do it." The answer should be "yes, but here is what requirement goes unverified, here is the failure mode we would be accepting, and here is the margin we are consuming." That is an engineering conversation.

Engineers who can have that conversation — who understand the rationale for every element of their test program — are the ones who maintain the integrity of the I&T campaign when it is under the most pressure.

Summary

The I&T Plan and the program architecture it defines are the foundation of the entire spacecraft integration and test campaign. A well-constructed architecture — one that addresses every level of integration from piece parts to observatory, phases the work correctly against the program lifecycle, defines a complete suite of governing documents, and plans explicitly for anomalies and schedule pressure — is the single most important determinant of I&T campaign success.

The levels of integration described in this article — piece parts, assemblies, components, subsystems, instruments, spacecraft, and observatory — are not administrative constructs. They reflect a fundamental physical reality: integration creates emergent behaviors that can only be found and resolved at the level where they emerge. Testing at every level is not redundancy. It is risk management.
