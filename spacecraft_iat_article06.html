Anomalies, Discrepancies,
and Problem Resolution

The formal discipline of finding, classifying, investigating, and closing the unexpected findings that every test programme generates — and the cultural conditions that determine whether that discipline works.

Every test programme generates anomalies. Not occasionally, not on poorly-managed programmes — every test programme, on every spacecraft, at every level of the integration hierarchy. The purpose of testing is precisely to find anomalies: unexpected behaviours, out-of-limit readings, hardware that does not perform as predicted. A test programme that generates no anomalies is not a well-executed test programme. It is a test programme that is not looking hard enough.

The question, therefore, is never whether anomalies will be found. The question is whether the programme has the discipline, the tools, and the culture to find all of them, document every one, investigate each to its true root cause, and resolve them in a way that justifies flight. Anomaly management is not a support function of the I&T programme — it is its central engineering discipline. The test campaigns described in the previous articles generate the data. Anomaly management is how the programme converts that data into justified confidence.

"The most dangerous anomaly is not the one that destroys hardware on the test floor. It is the one that is observed, briefly discussed, explained away as a test artefact — and never written down."

1. Why Anomaly Management Is the Central Discipline of I&T

To understand why anomaly management is so central, consider what a spacecraft programme is trying to achieve. It is trying to send a complex, one-of-a-kind piece of hardware to an environment it cannot return from, operated by a team that cannot physically access it, required to perform reliably for years or decades without maintenance. The programme's only tool for building justified confidence that this is possible is its test programme — and the only thing that separates a test programme from a series of scheduled operations is the rigour with which its anomalies are found and closed.

An anomaly found on the test floor and properly investigated and corrected adds directly to flight confidence. An anomaly that occurs on the test floor but is not documented, not investigated, and not corrected represents a risk that has been accepted — not explicitly, through an engineering assessment, but implicitly, through inattention. The history of spaceflight is filled with missions that were lost to anomalies observed on the ground, noted by someone as "probably nothing," and never formally addressed.

Anomaly management is also the primary mechanism by which a programme's engineering knowledge accumulates. A properly written discrepancy report, with a full root-cause investigation and a documented corrective action, is a permanent engineering record. The engineer who investigates a solder joint failure and documents the failure mode, the stress analysis, and the corrective redesign has created a record that will protect every subsequent spacecraft that uses the same component. The engineer who notes "connector re-seated, passed retest, closed" has created no such record — and the same failure will occur again.

2. The Discrepancy Report: Anatomy and Purpose

The Discrepancy Report (DR) — also called a Non-Conformance Report (NCR), Problem Report (PR), or Anomaly Report (AR) depending on the programme — is the formal document by which every anomaly is captured, tracked, and resolved. It is the engineering record of a problem, its investigation, and its resolution, and it will be reviewed by launch readiness boards and, potentially, by accident investigation boards if something goes wrong after launch.

Discrepancy Report Lifecycle — From Observation to Closure
Step 1
Observation & Initiation
Anomaly observed. Test hold called if required. DR opened immediately — never deferred. Initial observation recorded verbatim.
Step 2
Classification & Assignment
Severity category assigned. Responsible engineer assigned. Immediate containment actions identified if hardware is at risk.
Step 3
Root Cause Investigation
Systematic investigation to identify the true root cause. Failed hardware is preserved. Analysis and testing performed as required.
Step 4
Corrective Action
Corrective action identified and approved. May be repair, redesign, or formal acceptance. Fleet impact assessed and addressed.
Step 5
Verification & Closure
Action implemented and independently verified. Functional test confirms resolution. DR Board reviews and closes. Lessons learned captured.

The DR contains, at minimum: a precise, objective description of the observed anomaly; hardware identification and serial number; test procedure step at which the anomaly was observed; test configuration at the time; date, time, and names of personnel present; photographs or data plots; and the required resolution date. Vague initial descriptions — "anomalous reading on CH47" without context — are a common failure mode. The person who writes the initial entry must provide enough information for an engineer who was not present to understand exactly what was observed.

The "Never Deferred" Rule

The single most important procedural rule in DR management: a DR is opened at the time of observation, not later. "I'll write it up after the test" is the most common path to an undocumented anomaly. Test environments are busy, distracted environments; anomalies noted mentally and not written down are frequently forgotten, misremembered, or — under schedule pressure — rationalised away. The discipline is simple: if it was observed and it was unexpected, it gets a DR number before the test continues.

3. Anomaly Severity Classification

Not all anomalies carry equal risk, and the programme's response must be proportional to severity. A classification system ensures that critical anomalies receive the most immediate and thorough response, while routine workmanship issues do not consume engineering resources disproportionate to their risk.

Anomaly Severity Classification — Four-Category Framework
CAT
Definition
Programme Response
Closure Authority
CAT 1
Critical

Anomaly that could result in mission loss or spacecraft loss. Includes any functional failure in a non-redundant critical system, any anomaly with an unresolved flight safety implication, or any observation that cannot be plausibly explained by current engineering understanding.

Immediate test hold. All operations cease. Senior leadership notified. No further operations until root cause is understood and approved corrective action is in place. Independent review required.

Chief Engineer and Programme Manager, with concurrence from Independent Technical Authority. Cannot be closed by the engineering team alone.

CAT 2
Major

Significant departure from expected performance but without immediate mission risk. Includes out-of-limit readings in non-critical systems, functional anomalies with understood workarounds, and structural findings requiring analysis before flight impact can be assessed.

Test hold until initial assessment complete. Test may resume if chief engineer determines the anomaly does not affect hardware integrity. Full root-cause investigation required before launch.

Chief Engineer with DR Board concurrence. Resolution must be documented in the launch readiness matrix.

CAT 3
Minor

Anomaly within the engineering team's experience base and clearly not a flight risk. Includes workmanship defects within rework standards, minor calibration offsets, procedure clarifications, and test artefact readings positively identified as non-representative of flight hardware condition.

Test continues. Investigation assigned. Resolution required before the next major test milestone.

Responsible Systems Engineer with DR Board review.

CAT 4
Observation

An observation that is within specification but warrants documentation — for example, a component that consistently runs at the upper end of its temperature limit without exceeding it, or a connector that required more insertion force than expected.

Test continues. Observation recorded. No corrective action required unless subsequent data changes the assessment.

Responsible Systems Engineer. Reviewed at DR Board for trend analysis.

The severity classification must be assigned by a qualified engineer — typically the Systems Engineer or Chief Engineer — not by the technician or test operator who observed the anomaly. Anomalies that appear minor at first glance can turn out to be critical upon investigation, and the classification process exists to apply engineering judgment to this assessment consistently.

4. Root Cause Analysis — Methods and Disciplines

Root cause analysis is the systematic process of determining the fundamental reason why an anomaly occurred — not the symptoms, not the proximate cause, but the underlying cause that, if corrected, will prevent recurrence. It is the most intellectually demanding activity in the anomaly management process, and the one most commonly done inadequately under schedule pressure.

The most important principle: distinguish between the symptom and the cause. A connector that tested open is a symptom. A solder bridge that failed during vibration is a proximate cause. A process control deficiency that allowed a non-standard solder alloy to be used in that connector's assembly is a root cause. Correcting only the symptom — replacing the connector — does nothing to prevent the same failure in other connectors made by the same process.

❓
Five Whys
Iterative causal chain

Ask "why?" iteratively at each level of the causal chain until the root cause is reached — typically after five iterations. Simple, powerful, and applicable without specialised tools. Risk: analysis can stop too early at a proximate cause, or diverge into multiple causal branches that require a more structured method to manage.

🐟
Ishikawa Diagram
Cause categories

A structured diagram organising potential causes by category — Method, Machine, Material, Man, Measurement, Environment (the 6Ms). Useful for complex anomalies with multiple potential contributing causes, forcing the team to consider all cause categories rather than pursuing the most obvious explanation.

🌳
Fault Tree Analysis
Deductive top-down logic

A logic tree using AND/OR gates traces backward from the observed failure through all possible causal pathways. Particularly useful when multiple independent failures could each have caused the anomaly and the investigation must determine which occurred, or when the system has complex redundancy architecture.

🔬
Failure Analysis (FA)
Physical evidence

Physical laboratory analysis of the failed hardware — SEM/EDX, cross-section, X-ray, electrical characterisation — to identify the failure mechanism directly from evidence. The gold standard for hardware failures where the failed article is available. The failure analysis lab is the final arbiter when analytical methods produce conflicting theories.

📊
Trend Analysis
Pattern across the fleet

Review of all DRs across the programme — and similar programmes — to identify patterns not visible when each anomaly is investigated in isolation. A single connector failure may be coincidence. Five connector failures on the same connector family across two programmes is a systemic issue. Trend analysis catches systemic issues before they become mission failures.

Preserving the Evidence

  • Do not touch, move, or disturb failed hardware before photographing it in situ and initiating a DR.
  • If hardware must be moved to prevent further damage, document its condition fully before moving and tag it clearly as anomalous hardware requiring failure analysis.
  • Do not perform any rework on failed hardware until the failure analysis is complete and the root cause is identified.
  • Preserve all test data from the period before and during the anomaly. Data recordings, operator logs, and environmental monitoring records must be archived and protected from accidental deletion.

5. Corrective Action, Verification, and Closure

The output of a root cause investigation is a corrective action — the specific engineering action taken to address the root cause and prevent recurrence. The corrective action must be commensurate with the root cause: a workmanship defect on a single article requires rework of that article; a design deficiency requires redesign of all affected hardware; a process deficiency requires correction of the process and assessment of all hardware built under the deficient process.

Corrective Action Type When Applied Verification Method Fleet Impact
Hardware Rework Workmanship defect on a specific article; design change is not required Post-rework inspection to workmanship standards; functional retest through the affected test sequence Assess other articles built under the same process for similar defects
Design Change Root cause is a design deficiency that will produce the same failure regardless of workmanship quality Analysis of revised design; environmental and functional retest of redesigned hardware All flight articles using the affected design must be assessed; some or all may require retrofit
Process Change Root cause is in the manufacturing, assembly, or test process rather than the hardware design Process audit confirming changed process is correctly implemented; sample inspection of new hardware All hardware built under the old process must be assessed; 100% inspection or retest may be required
Re-inspection / Re-test Root cause is understood; hardware requires additional verification to confirm condition The inspection or test that provides the required verification, performed to a defined acceptance criterion May apply to all units of the same type if the concern is about inspection or test adequacy
Formal Accept As-Is Non-conformance is understood and determined through engineering analysis to be acceptable for flight Engineering analysis documented and approved through the formal waiver process Documented in the flight discrepancy record and launch readiness matrix; monitored for recurrence

Corrective action verification is not optional. The verification must demonstrate, through objective evidence, that the corrective action achieved its intended result. For a hardware rework, that means post-rework inspection followed by functional testing through the relevant test sequence. "We reworked it and it looked fine" is not a verification.

6. The DR Board and Anomaly Review Process

Individual DRs are managed by the responsible engineer, but the overall anomaly management programme is governed by the Discrepancy Review Board (DRB). The DRB is a standing body that meets regularly — typically weekly during active test campaigns, more frequently when critical anomalies are open — to review the status of all open DRs, assess new DRs for severity classification, and approve closures.

The DRB membership must include the Chief Engineer or designated representative (who owns the classification and closure decisions), Systems Engineers for each major subsystem (the domain experts), a Quality Assurance representative (the independent audit function), and a Mission Assurance representative (for flight safety considerations). On government-funded programmes, the customer's technical authority typically has observer or voting status on significant dispositions and waivers.

The DRB must function as a technical body, not a schedule management body. The most common pathology of DRBs under programme schedule pressure is the gradual shift from "is this anomaly understood and resolved?" to "can we close this DR to keep the schedule on track?" These are different questions with different answers, and confusing them is the first step toward normalisation of deviance.

7. Waivers, Deviations, and the Acceptance of Non-Conformance

Not every anomaly can be corrected. Some non-conformances cannot be repaired without replacing hardware that is not replaceable, or redesigning systems with insufficient time before launch, or retesting hardware that has already consumed most of its fatigue life. In these cases, the programme must formally accept the non-conformance as a known, documented risk.

  • A waiver is a post-event formal acceptance that a specific piece of hardware does not meet a specified requirement, and that this non-conformance is acceptable for the intended mission. A waiver requires a technical justification, approved by the programme's technical authority and, typically, by the customer's independent technical authority. Waivers apply to specific hardware articles, not across the fleet.
  • A deviation is a pre-approved departure from a requirement, granted before hardware is built or a test is performed. Deviations address known limitations in the test environment or manufacturing process — a requirement that cannot be directly verified on the ground, for example — and must be approved before the fact with justification for why the programme can still meet its mission objectives.

The critical discipline of waiver management is to assess the combined effect of all waivers on the programme's overall risk posture, not each waiver in isolation. Individually, each waiver may be justifiable. Collectively, ten waivers on different subsystems may represent an unacceptable accumulation of risk that no single waiver review captured. This is one of the primary responsibilities of the chief engineer: to maintain visibility of the total waiver burden and assess its integrated effect on flight confidence.

⚠ The Waiver Accumulation Problem

On one large programme, we reached the pre-ship review with seventeen open waivers across the various subsystems. Each had been individually reviewed and approved by the responsible subsystem engineer and the DR Board. Each had been individually assessed as acceptable for flight. But no one had looked at all seventeen together and asked: what does this collection of known departures from specification mean for the overall probability of mission success?

We spent three days at the pre-ship review doing that integrated assessment. Two of the seventeen waivers, when considered together, revealed a cold-case power budget shortfall during a specific orbital geometry that neither waiver analysis had addressed individually. We caught it. An operational change to the mission profile resolved it. The spacecraft flew successfully. But that was a close call that a proper waiver integration review conducted earlier would have caught months before the pre-ship review.

8. The Open DR List — Managing the Residual Risk Ledger

At any point during the I&T programme, there will be a number of open DRs — anomalies identified but not yet fully resolved. This list is not a list of failures. It is the programme's residual risk ledger: a formal, tracked record of every known departure from expected performance, every open investigation, every corrective action not yet verified.

Open →

Anomaly documented, awaiting investigation or corrective action. Must have a responsible engineer and a required resolution date.

In Work ⚙

Root cause investigation actively in progress. Hardware may be in failure analysis. Interim corrective action may be in place.

Closed ✓

Root cause identified, corrective action verified, DR Board approved. Part of the permanent test record; trended for pattern analysis.

Waived W

Non-conformance formally accepted through the waiver process. Root cause understood. Flight impact assessed and accepted by technical authority.

The open DR list is a launch readiness input. No spacecraft launches with unreviewed open DRs — every open DR must be formally presented to and accepted by the launch readiness review board, with documented technical rationale for why the open anomaly does not affect flight safety or mission success. This is the last formal checkpoint at which the entire open anomaly burden is reviewed by the programme's technical leadership with the explicit question: do we have enough confidence to commit this hardware to launch?

9. Normalisation of Deviance — The Most Dangerous Failure Mode

Of all the failure modes in anomaly management, the most insidious is what sociologist Diane Vaughan, in her analysis of the Space Shuttle Challenger accident, called the normalisation of deviance: the gradual process by which anomalous conditions come to be treated as normal and acceptable through repeated exposure without apparent consequence.

Normalisation of deviance does not happen through dishonesty or negligence. It happens through a sequence of individually reasonable decisions under conditions of incomplete information and schedule pressure. The mechanics are consistent across programmes:

StageWhat HappensThe Rationalisation
First Occurrence An anomaly is observed that is outside expectations but within specification limits. The team investigates, does not find a clear root cause, and decides to proceed. "It's within spec. We understand it well enough. The schedule doesn't allow for a deeper investigation right now."
Second Occurrence The same anomaly recurs. Because the first was accepted without root cause, this second occurrence is treated as confirmation that the anomaly is "normal for this hardware." "We saw this last time. It's just how this hardware behaves. We know it's safe because the previous unit was fine."
Institutionalisation The anomaly is no longer documented as a DR. It is treated as an "expected behaviour," noted — if at all — as a comment in the test log. "This is a known characteristic. Everyone knows about it. There's no point raising a DR — it just creates paperwork."
Boundary Expansion A more severe occurrence is observed. The same rationalisation is applied: "we've seen worse and it was fine." "This is slightly more than usual, but we know this hardware is robust. We've been living with this for months."
Failure The anomaly, never understood because never formally investigated, produces a failure in test or in flight, traceable through years of records to the first occurrence accepted without root cause. "We should have known. The signs were there. We just stopped seeing them."
⚙ Countering Normalisation — Structural Protections

Mandatory DR for every anomaly: No exceptions. Every anomaly gets a DR number. The act of writing it down prevents the gradual cognitive process by which it becomes invisible.

Trend analysis across the DR database: Periodically, the QA team and chief engineer review the full DR history looking for repeated anomalies in the same hardware, same process, or same test campaign. Individually accepted anomalies may, in aggregate, reveal a pattern demanding deeper investigation.

Independent technical review: A team that has been living with a set of anomalies for months cannot reliably identify which ones have been normalised. Periodic independent reviews by engineers who have not been part of the day-to-day test programme are a structural protection against team-level normalisation.

Stop-work authority at every level: Every member of the test team — from the chief engineer to the most junior technician — must have the explicit authority and genuine cultural permission to stop a test when something does not look right. Environments where stopping work is implicitly punished through comments about schedule impact will produce normalised deviance.

10. Lessons from Flight Anomalies and Failures

The history of spacecraft I&T anomaly management is a record of hard-won lessons. The following examples are selected not for their drama but for how clearly they illustrate the principles described in this article.

1967
Apollo 1
Over 100 open DRs were documented during the Apollo 1 spacecraft test programme, many related to wire bundles and cabin materials. The programme proceeded to a launch pad test with the cabin pressurised in pure oxygen at above-ambient pressure — a configuration never formally risk-assessed as a test configuration. A cabin fire killed three astronauts. The investigation found that the normalisation of a growing workmanship defect count, and the failure to treat the pressurised pure-oxygen cabin as requiring specific hazard controls, were key contributing factors.
1986
Space Shuttle Challenger
O-ring erosion had been observed on multiple previous flights. Each occurrence was reviewed, accepted as within the engineering database, and used as justification for proceeding rather than as evidence of a design deficiency. The night before launch, engineers argued against launch in cold temperatures. The decision-making process had normalised O-ring erosion to the point where the absence of catastrophe in previous flights was interpreted as evidence of acceptable risk. The O-ring failed on the 25th mission.
1998
Mars Climate Orbiter
A navigation anomaly — unexpectedly large trajectory corrections — was flagged during cruise. The anomaly was investigated to an intermediate level; the root cause (a unit mismatch between a contractor software module using imperial units and the flight system expecting metric units) was not identified. The spacecraft entered the Mars atmosphere at the wrong angle and was lost. Multiple opportunities to identify and resolve the anomaly before arrival were available but not fully pursued.
2003
Space Shuttle Columbia
Foam shedding from the external tank had been observed on previous flights and progressively normalised over many years, documented but treated as "in-family" events not meeting the standard for formal corrective action. The Columbia Accident Investigation Board found that the shedding process was not understood — the wing leading edge was more vulnerable than analytically assessed — and that repeated acceptance without root cause had created a false baseline of safety.
2021
James Webb Space Telescope
JWST's I&T programme is notable for sustained anomaly management rigour over more than two decades. Hundreds of DRs were generated, investigated, and formally resolved. Several significant anomalies — a sunshield membrane tear during deployment testing, a communications anomaly during launch site processing — were caught, investigated to root cause, and corrected. The open DR list at the launch readiness review contained zero unreviewed anomalies. JWST reached operational orbit and began science operations successfully.

11. What Good Anomaly Management Looks Like

Good anomaly management tends to be invisible: the spacecraft launches and succeeds, and no one outside the programme knows how many anomalies were found, investigated, and resolved in the years leading up to that launch. But for those who work in I&T, the characteristics are recognisable:

  • A high DR count is a sign of health, not failure. Programmes with active, thorough test campaigns generate large numbers of DRs. The number of DRs is a measure of test thoroughness, not of programme quality. A programme generating very few DRs is either not testing rigorously enough, or not documenting what it finds, or both.
  • The DR database is a knowledge repository, not a paperwork burden. In well-managed programmes, engineers routinely search the database to understand the failure history of a component or process before committing to a test or design decision. The database is treated as engineering intelligence.
  • Anomalies are discussed in technical terms, not schedule terms. In the DRB meetings of well-managed programmes, the conversation is about root causes and failure mechanisms. Schedule implications are addressed after the technical assessment, not before. Programmes where every DRB meeting begins with "how does this affect the schedule?" have inverted the priority.
  • Every anomaly gets a root cause. Not just the big ones. "Unknown" is not an acceptable root cause for a closed DR on a flight programme.
  • The chief engineer knows the open DR list by heart. On well-managed programmes, the chief engineer can at any moment describe from memory the open DRs of concern — their status, the open questions, the resolution plans. This is a consequence of the level of technical engagement that good chief engineers maintain as a matter of professional discipline.

"Every anomaly you find on the test floor is a gift. It is a problem revealed at a point where you can still do something about it. Treat it with gratitude, with rigour, and with the full resources of the programme."

Summary

Anomaly management is the discipline that gives the I&T programme its engineering meaning. Tests generate data; anomaly management converts that data into justified confidence. The mechanisms — the Discrepancy Report, the severity classification system, root cause analysis, corrective action and verification, the DR Board, and the formal waiver process — are the instruments of that conversion. The pathology to guard against in every programme, at every phase, is the normalisation of deviance: the gradual drift from rigorous investigation to comfortable acceptance of the unexplained.
