Quality assurance in spacecraft I&T is the systematic set of activities that confirm the spacecraft is being built, assembled, and tested in accordance with its design requirements and workmanship standards — that the work being done is the right work, performed correctly, by qualified people, using approved materials and procedures, documented in a way that creates an auditable record. It is not a separate activity performed after engineering is done. It is woven into every step of the I&T programme, from the qualification of the assembly technician's soldering technique to the independent witness signature on the traveller to the customer representative standing behind the test conductor at every critical test.

"Quality is not inspected in. Quality is built in. The inspector who finds a defect has found evidence that quality was not built in. The inspector who finds nothing has not proven that quality is present — only that no defect was visible in the time and access available for inspection. The goal of quality assurance is to make defects impossible, not merely to detect them."

1. What Quality Assurance Is — and What It Is Not

Quality assurance (QA) is the set of planned, systematic activities necessary to provide adequate confidence that a product will satisfy given requirements for quality. It encompasses the definition of quality standards, the training and qualification of the people who perform quality-critical work, the design and implementation of inspection and verification activities, the investigation and dispositioning of non-conformances, and the auditing of quality systems to confirm they are functioning as intended.

Quality assurance is not the same as quality control. Quality control (QC) is the subset of QA activities specifically focused on detecting defects in products — inspection, testing, and measurement. QA is the broader discipline that includes the processes and systems that prevent defects from occurring in the first place. A programme with strong QC and weak QA will detect defects after they occur, at increasing cost as the programme progresses. A programme with strong QA — robust processes, qualified people, and a culture in which quality is understood as a shared responsibility — will have fewer defects to detect.

📋
Prevention

The QA activities that prevent defects from occurring: process qualification, technician training and certification, approved procedure development, workmanship standards definition, material certification. The highest-value QA activities. Their success is invisible — prevented defects leave no trace in the record.

🔍
Detection

The QA activities that find defects before they affect mission performance: in-process inspection, first article inspection, source inspection at suppliers, receiving inspection, and the various audit activities that confirm quality systems are functioning. Detection activities generate the discrepancy records that feed into the anomaly management system.

🔧
Disposition

The QA activities that ensure defects, once found, are correctly dispositioned: the Non-Conformance Report (NCR) process, the Material Review Board (MRB), root cause analysis, corrective action implementation, and verification that the corrective action is effective. Disposition is not complete when the defect is repaired — it is complete when the root cause has been identified and the process that allowed the defect to occur has been corrected.

📊
Improvement

The QA activities that make the programme progressively better: trend analysis of NCRs and DRs to identify systemic quality problems, process audits that reveal systemic weaknesses before they produce defects, lessons learned from completed programmes applied to current practice. The discipline that converts experience into institutional knowledge.

2. The QA Organisation — Structure, Independence, and Authority

The quality assurance organisation must be independent of the engineering and production organisations whose work it is reviewing. This independence is not an organisational formality — it is the structural safeguard against the pressure that every programme experiences to accept marginal work, close anomalies without adequate investigation, and approve waivers that should not be approved when schedule and cost are under pressure.

In practice, QA independence means that the QA manager reports to the programme manager or to the company's quality director — not to the chief engineer or the I&T manager. The QA organisation has the authority to withhold approval of work, to require repeat inspections, to issue hold tags that stop work on specific operations, and to escalate quality concerns to the programme manager without going through the engineering management chain. These authorities are only meaningful if they are actually exercised — a QA organisation that always concurs with engineering judgements under schedule pressure is not independent in any operationally meaningful sense.

4. Mandatory Inspection Points and Hold Points

Not all inspection points in a procedure are equal. A Mandatory Inspection Point (MIP) is a designated step in a procedure where a QA inspector must be physically present and must independently verify the specified condition before the work can proceed. The inspector's signature is required in the traveller at the MIP. If the inspector is not available, work stops until the inspector arrives — the MIP is not satisfied by a retrospective inspection after the operation has continued.

A Hold Point is a more restrictive designation: work stops at the hold point and cannot proceed until a specified authority — which may be a QA inspector, a lead engineer, a customer representative, or a programme manager — has reviewed the work and given formal written authorisation to continue. Hold points are used for the highest-consequence operations: the first installation of an instrument on the spacecraft, the first pressurisation of the propulsion system, the first powered-on functional test with all subsystems integrated.

⚙ The Value of the Unannounced Inspector

A QA programme whose inspectors always arrive exactly when scheduled, review exactly what was planned for review, and sign off within predictable time windows is a programme whose inspection activity has become routine — and routine inspection tends to find routine defects while missing non-routine ones. The most effective QA programmes supplement scheduled inspection with random, unannounced quality surveillance: a QA inspector who walks the floor unscheduled, observes work in progress without prior arrangement, and reports what is actually happening rather than what the programme thought would be happening when the inspection schedule was written.

Unannounced surveillance is not a distrust of the workforce — it is recognition that people behave differently when observed, and that the work environment when the QA inspector is expected differs from the work environment the rest of the time. Programmes that discover this difference through random surveillance have the opportunity to correct it. Programmes that never look are confident that the work environment is always as it appears during scheduled inspections. That confidence is not always warranted.

5. Workmanship Standards — The Foundation of Quality

Workmanship standards define the visual and measurable criteria that distinguish acceptable work from unacceptable work for every production process in the programme — soldering, crimping, bonding, potting, wire harnessing, conformal coating, mechanical assembly, and torquing. They provide the inspector and the technician with a common reference: when both parties are working from the same standard, the inspection is objective, not a negotiation between different subjective judgements of what "acceptable" means.

For electronic assembly and wiring harness work, the primary standards for US government space programmes are NASA-STD-8739.3 (Soldered Electrical Connections), NASA-STD-8739.4 (Crimping, Interconnecting Cables, Harnesses, and Wiring), and the IPC/WHMA-A-620 standard (Requirements and Acceptance for Cable and Wire Harness Assemblies). These standards define, with photographs and dimensional criteria, the acceptance and rejection criteria for every common workmanship condition — solder joint shape and wetting, insulation clearance, conductor braid coverage, splice configuration, connector pin seating, and hundreds of other details that would otherwise be left to individual judgement.

Technician Certification

Workmanship standards are only effective if the people performing the work are trained to the standard and certified as proficient before they perform work on flight hardware. Technician certification is the formal process by which a technician demonstrates, by training and by practical examination, that they can perform a specific process — soldering, crimping, conformal coating application — to the required standard. Certification is process-specific and time-limited: a technician certified to solder is not thereby certified to crimp; and a certification that was current three years ago may have lapsed if the technician has not performed the process regularly and undergone re-certification.

No uncertified person performs quality-critical work on flight hardware. This is an absolute requirement with no schedule exception. A programme that allows uncertified work on flight hardware to meet a schedule constraint has accepted a quality risk that it has not characterised and cannot bound — because the fundamental premise of the workmanship standard, that the person performing the work has demonstrated they can meet the standard, has been removed.

6. First Article Inspection and Process Qualification

When a new manufacturing process is introduced — a new adhesive formulation, a new potting compound, a new conformal coating chemistry, a new soldering flux — it must be qualified before it is used on flight hardware. Process qualification demonstrates, by test, that the process produces results that meet the required workmanship standards and that the process is repeatable and controllable within the defined parameters. A process qualification specimen — test articles made using the new process — is subjected to the relevant environmental exposures (thermal cycling, humidity, vibration) and inspected to confirm that the process performs as required.

Similarly, when a new design — a new circuit board layout, a new structural assembly, a new harness configuration — is first built, it is subject to a First Article Inspection (FAI): a comprehensive dimensional and documentation review that confirms the first article conforms to every requirement of the design before subsequent articles are built to the same design. The FAI is the only inspection that verifies every dimensional requirement; subsequent acceptance inspections for production articles focus on the most critical characteristics and on any deviations from the first article.

7. Source Inspection and Supplier Quality

A spacecraft programme builds its hardware from components supplied by dozens or hundreds of external suppliers — electronic component manufacturers, structural machining shops, harness assembly contractors, instrument subsystem developers. The quality of those components when they arrive at the integration facility depends entirely on the quality management practices of each supplier. Source inspection — the practice of sending programme QA representatives to the supplier's facility to witness critical manufacturing operations and inspect completed articles before shipment — is the primary tool for verifying that supplier quality is what the programme requires.

Source inspection is not always practical or cost-effective for every supplier and every component. The programme's supplier quality management plan defines, based on the criticality of each component and the risk profile of each supplier (driven by supplier audit results, historical quality performance, and process complexity), which components require source inspection and at what level. A new supplier with no programme history providing a safety-critical component should expect resident QA oversight. An established supplier with a strong quality record providing a standard commercial component may need only receiving inspection at the integration facility.

◆ The Counterfeit Parts Problem

One of the most insidious supplier quality threats in modern aerospace programmes is the introduction of counterfeit electronic components — components labelled as conforming to a specific military or space-grade specification but manufactured to a lower standard, or re-marked commercial components presented as higher-grade equivalents. Counterfeit parts have been found in aerospace supply chains at multiple levels: at electronics distributors, at subcontractors, and occasionally at the integration facility.

The consequences of a counterfeit part in a spacecraft are potentially severe. A component labelled as radiation-hardened that is actually a commercial grade will fail much sooner in the radiation environment of space than its specification would suggest. A component labelled as operating to a wider temperature range than it actually does will fail during thermal-vacuum testing — if the test programme is rigorous enough to expose it. Or it will fail on orbit, under conditions the test programme did not reach.

Counterfeit parts are mitigated through approved source purchasing (buying only from the component manufacturer or authorised distributors), incoming component authentication testing (electrical testing and, for high-risk components, physical cross-section analysis and X-ray inspection), and supply chain audit programmes that verify the distributor's procurement and storage practices. Programmes that purchase components from non-authorised distributors to meet schedule or cost constraints are accepting a quality risk that cannot be fully mitigated by incoming inspection alone.

8. The Stop-Work Authority

The most powerful quality assurance tool — and the one most rarely used and most important when it is — is the stop-work authority: the power of a QA inspector, a quality engineer, or any programme employee to halt work on the spacecraft when they observe a condition that poses an unacceptable quality risk, until that condition is resolved.

Stop-work authority is distinct from the DR system. A DR documents an anomaly for investigation and dispositioning. Stop-work authority prevents further work — which might make the anomaly worse, or might make it undetectable, or might compromise the integrity of the investigation — until the responsible parties have assessed the situation. A stop-work call is not a career-limiting act of insubordination. In a healthy quality culture, it is evidence of the kind of individual vigilance that the programme depends on.

9. Customer and Independent Oversight

For government-sponsored programmes — NASA, ESA, and DoD missions — the funding agency exercises its own independent oversight of the programme's quality management system, in addition to the programme's internal QA activities. This oversight takes several forms, each of which serves a different purpose.

  • Government Quality Assurance Representative (GQAR) or mission assurance representative. A customer-designated quality representative who is present at the programme on a regular basis — monthly, weekly, or resident — to witness critical operations, review quality records, attend programme reviews, and report independently to the customer on the programme's quality status. The GQAR is not part of the programme team and is not subject to programme management authority. The GQAR's observations and concerns go directly to the customer.
  • Independent readiness assessments. Customer-directed technical reviews at major programme milestones — often at the system-level test readiness review, the pre-ship review, and the launch readiness review — in which customer engineers independently assess the programme's readiness to proceed. These assessments may focus on quality and workmanship, on anomaly closure, or on the completeness of the verification matrix, depending on the customer's programme insight requirements.
  • Quality audits. Formal, documented reviews of the programme's quality management system against a defined standard — typically the programme's own Quality Management Plan, supplemented by applicable NASA or ESA quality standards. Audits evaluate whether the quality system is being implemented as documented, whether the documented system is adequate for the programme's needs, and whether there are systemic quality issues visible in the programme's records. Audit findings are formal programme-level action items.
  • Failure Review Board (FRB) participation. For significant anomalies — anomalies that result in hardware damage, that reveal systemic quality problems, or that occur during safety-critical operations — the customer may convene or participate in a Failure Review Board that investigates the root cause, evaluates the corrective action, and formally closes the anomaly. The FRB's findings and recommendations are authoritative: the programme cannot close the anomaly without the FRB's concurrence.

10. Quality Metrics — What to Measure and What They Mean

Quality metrics — quantitative measures of the programme's quality performance — serve two purposes: they provide a current snapshot of quality status, and they reveal trends that may indicate systemic problems before those problems produce catastrophic defects. The right metrics are those that measure something meaningful about the quality of the work being done, not merely the activity of the quality organisation.

NCR/unit

Non-conformance reports per unit of production — a normalised measure of defect rate that allows comparison across programmes and over time within a programme

SCRR

Scheduled vs. Completed Re-Inspection Rate — the fraction of required re-inspections after rework that are completed on schedule; a measure of rework cycle discipline

Open NCR Age

The distribution of ages of open non-conformance reports — NCRs that remain open for extended periods indicate dispositioning bottlenecks or systemic quality problems that are not being resolved

Repeat NCR %

The fraction of NCRs that represent a defect type seen before — a high repeat NCR rate indicates that corrective actions are not effective or are not being implemented

Trends in these metrics are more important than point-in-time values. A programme whose NCR rate is declining as integration proceeds is demonstrating that its quality processes are improving — as the workforce becomes more experienced with the specific procedures, materials, and standards of this programme. A programme whose NCR rate is increasing as it approaches the end of integration may be experiencing schedule pressure that is compromising quality practices. Both trends are visible in the metrics before they produce a crisis — if the metrics are reviewed regularly and honestly, and if the programme leadership is willing to act on what the metrics reveal.

11. Quality Culture — The Difference That Cannot Be Audited

Every QA system described in this article — the inspection procedures, the workmanship standards, the certifications, the stop-work authority, the customer oversight — can be documented, audited, and demonstrated to be formally in place. None of it, by itself, makes a spacecraft of high quality. The spacecraft is made of high quality by the people who build and test it, and by the culture in which they work — the culture that determines whether those people treat the documented requirements as meaningful constraints on their work or as bureaucratic obstacles to be navigated around when schedule and cost are under pressure.

Quality culture is the aggregate of individual attitudes, team norms, management behaviours, and organisational incentives that determine how quality is actually prioritised when it conflicts with schedule, cost, or technical convenience. It cannot be audited directly, because audits observe documented practices, not the attitudes that determine how those practices are applied in the moments that matter — the moment a technician decides whether to report a marginal solder joint or move on; the moment a test engineer decides whether to investigate an anomaly that might delay the test or to attribute it to a known benign cause; the moment a manager decides whether to approve a waiver that should require more analysis or to close it to clear the schedule.

Characteristics of a Genuine Quality Culture
1
Anyone Can Stop the Work
Any person — technician, engineer, QA inspector, new graduate — who observes a quality concern has both the right and the obligation to raise it, without fear of management pressure, ridicule, or career consequences. Programmes where only senior people raise quality concerns are programmes where quality concerns are being suppressed.
2
Anomalies Are Assets, Not Embarrassments
A programme that finds many anomalies during ground testing is not a programme with poor quality — it is a programme with a rigorous test programme that is finding problems before they become on-orbit failures. A programme with suspiciously few anomalies should examine whether the test programme is rigorous enough to find them, not congratulate itself on its quality.
3
Schedule Never Overrides Safety or Quality
This principle is easy to affirm in a programme review and difficult to uphold at 11 PM the night before a major milestone, when a borderline quality finding is the only thing standing between the programme and a week's schedule delay. The programmes that uphold this principle in those moments — consistently, visibly, with management endorsement — are the programmes that arrive at launch with justified confidence in their spacecraft.
4
Root Cause, Not Blame
When a defect occurs, the quality culture's response is to understand why — what in the process, the training, the procedure, or the work environment allowed the defect to occur — and to fix that, not to find and punish an individual. Blame culture suppresses anomaly reporting. Root cause culture creates the conditions in which anomalies are reported promptly, investigated thoroughly, and corrected systemically.
5
Quality Is Everybody's Job
The quality organisation is responsible for the quality management system. The quality of the spacecraft is the responsibility of every person who touches it — engineers who write procedures that are actually executable, technicians who perform work that meets the standard, managers who create the conditions in which quality is achievable. A QA organisation that carries the entire burden of quality for a programme that does not share that burden is a QA organisation that is being asked to do the impossible.

The Columbia Accident Investigation Board, reviewing the loss of Space Shuttle Columbia in 2003, identified NASA's safety culture as a root cause alongside the physical foam strike that breached the orbiter's heat shield. The CAIB found that management practices had allowed concerns raised by engineers — about the foam strike, about the imaging needed to assess damage, about the risk of re-entry with unknown thermal protection damage — to be suppressed or overridden by schedule and operational momentum. The physical failure mode was foam. The enabling condition was culture.

This is not a unique finding. Every major spacecraft failure investigation eventually reaches a cultural conclusion — a point at which the analysis moves from the physical failure to the organisational practices that allowed the physical failure to go undetected, unreported, or uncorrected. Quality culture is not a soft supplement to quality engineering. It is its essential foundation.
